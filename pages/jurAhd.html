The Journey Ahead

Preparing for pregnancy


Are you planning to have a baby?
here, is some information that will be useful for you during your journey ahead.


Why is planning your pregnancy important? ▼

Planning your pregnancy is important because it helps ensure that both you and your baby are healthy. When pregnancies are planned, there is a better chance of having a healthy baby. Planning allows you to take steps to get yourself in good health before getting pregnant and to receive regular medical care throughout your pregnancy, which is called prenatal care. Prenatal care is important for the well-being of both you and your baby.


How do you plan your pregnancy? ▼

In Sri Lanka, planning your pregnancy is just as important as anywhere else. By making a reproductive life plan, you can think about when and if you want to have a baby. Consider how many children you want and how far apart you'd like them to be.

Before getting pregnant, it's crucial to focus on your health. Make sure you are in good physical condition to have a healthy pregnancy and baby. If you're sexually active but not ready for a baby right now, it's essential to use effective contraception or birth control to avoid unplanned pregnancies.

Talk openly with your partner about your reproductive life plan. Remember that there are no right or wrong answers, and your plans may change as you both get older. It's okay if you don't agree on everything at first; take some time to discuss and decide together.

To prevent pregnancies until you are ready, explore the various

birth control options available in Sri Lanka. These could include intrauterine devices (IUDs), implants, the pill, or condoms. Speak with a healthcare provider who can help you choose the best birth control method for you and your partner's needs.

By planning your pregnancy and taking care of your health, you can increase the chances of having a healthy baby and a positive pregnancy experience.


Are you ready to be a parent? ▼

Becoming a parent brings significant changes to your life. Before having a baby, it's important to have a conversation with your partner (if you have one) to ensure you are both ready for the upcoming changes.

Here are some questions to consider:

  • Why do you want to have a baby? Do you feel pressured, or is it something you both genuinely want? Do you think you will enjoy being parents?

  • How will your relationship with your partner change when you have a baby? Are you both prepared for these changes?

  • How will having a baby impact your work or collage commitments?

  • Have you thought about child care? Will one of you stay at home to take care of the baby, or will you need to find daycare services?

  • Do you have any religious or cultural traditions you want to pass on to your child? Do you and your partner agree on these traditions?

  • Are you prepared to care for a baby who might be sick or have special needs?

  • Are you ready for the financial and personal sacrifices that come with having a baby, such as having less money and free time for yourself?

If you don't have a partner, ask yourself these questions to determine if you are ready to be a parent on your own. Taking the time to consider these factors can help you make an informed decision about whether you are ready to embark on the journey of parenthood.


Are you physically ready to get pregnant? ▼

Preparing your body before getting pregnant is important for a healthy pregnancy and baby. One way to start is by having a checkup with a healthcare provider before trying to conceive. This checkup ensures that you're in good health and ready for pregnancy. If you're not ready yet, your provider can help you choose and use birth control methods to prevent pregnancy until you're prepared.


Are you financially ready for a baby? ▼

Having a baby can be expensive, as there are many things to consider, such as diapers and child care costs. It's important to think about how having a baby might affect your finances. Here are a few steps you can take:

  • Create a budget:
    Write down all your monthly expenses, including housing, transportation, food, clothes, and medical bills. Compare your total expenses to your income. See how much money is left after paying your bills. You may need to reduce your spending in certain areas to have enough money for the things your baby will need.

  • Start saving:
    It's never too early to start saving for your baby. Consider the costs of child care and baby essentials and try to save some money each month towards these expenses.

  • Be a smart shopper:
    While some baby items may need to be new for safety reasons, you can buy certain things used or borrow them from friends and family. Just make sure that items like car seats and cribs meet the latest safety standards. You can visit the Consumer Affairs Authority (CAA) website to learn about safety standards and product recalls.

By creating a budget, saving money, and being a smart shopper, you can better prepare yourself financially for the arrival of your baby.


What do I need to do before I get pregnant? ▼

Before you get pregnant, there are important steps you can take to ensure a healthy pregnancy for you and your future baby. One of the first things you can do is visit a doctor or a local healthcare center, for pre-pregnancy care.

During pre-pregnancy care, the doctor or nurse will assess your overall health and address any concerns that may affect your pregnancy. They will ask you questions about your medical history, family medical history, current health issues, diet, lifestyle, and medications or supplements you may be taking. They will also discuss your pregnancy history, safety at home and work, and any mental health concerns.

As part of your pre-pregnancy care, the doctor may recommend a visit to the dentist for a checkup. Treating any gum disease before pregnancy can help prevent health problems for both you and your future baby.

Pre-pregnancy visits are an excellent opportunity to ask any questions you may have, so make sure to come prepared with any concerns or uncertainties you want to discuss. The healthcare provider will provide guidance and tips on how to improve your chances of getting pregnant and having a healthy pregnancy.


Do I need to worry about having a high-risk pregnancy? ▼

Having a high-risk pregnancy is uncommon, so there's no need to worry too much. While some things during pregnancy are beyond your control and can happen randomly, there are steps you can take to minimize risks and ensure a healthy pregnancy.

Getting pre-pregnancy care from a healthcare provider, such as a nurse, doctor, or local health center like Planned Parenthood, before getting pregnant is important. Additionally, attending regular prenatal care visits throughout your pregnancy can help lower the chances of having a high-risk pregnancy.

Certain conditions or factors can make pregnancy more challenging. If you have high blood pressure, heart or kidney disease, chronic conditions like diabetes or lupus, a history of miscarriages or premature births, or if you're aware of a risk of birth defects or genetic disorders, it becomes even more crucial to seek pre-pregnancy care.

Other factors that may require extra attention include having a sexually transmitted infection, being underweight or overweight, or being older than 35.

By receiving appropriate care and monitoring, you can increase the likelihood of a healthy and successful pregnancy. Remember, it's important to consult with a healthcare provider who can provide personalized advice based on your specific situation.


What’s my partner’s role in planning my pregnancy? ▼

Your partner can play a significant role in planning your pregnancy if you want them to. Their encouragement and emotional support can be very helpful, especially if you're making lifestyle changes to prepare for pregnancy.

It's important to consider your partner's health as well when it comes to getting pregnant. Certain factors can lower sperm count and affect semen quality, making it more difficult to conceive. These factors include drinking alcohol, smoking or using tobacco, using steroids or illegal drugs, taking certain prescription drugs or over-the-counter medicines, spending extended time in hot environments (like hot tubs or saunas), and having an unhealthy diet.

Having a conversation with your partner about making healthy lifestyle choices can benefit both their overall health and increase the chances of a successful pregnancy in the future. Working together as a team can help create a supportive and conducive environment for your pregnancy plans.


Do I need a new doctor once I get pregnant? ▼

If you already have a gynecologist, midwife, or family doctor whom you trust and feel comfortable with, you may be able to continue seeing them throughout your pregnancy. However, if you prefer to switch to a different healthcare provider, you can ask for recommendations from friends, family, or other healthcare professionals. Your local health center can also provide prenatal care or help you find a doctor in your area.

It's essential that you have a positive and comfortable relationship with your doctor or midwife, as you will be discussing your preferences for pregnancy and birth with them. Before making a decision, you can consider interviewing potential healthcare providers to ensure they align with your expectations. Remember, it's perfectly okay to switch to a new healthcare provider if you feel more at ease and supported by someone else.






Ovulation

Some important facts you should know.


What are the signs of ovulation? ▼

Here are some signs that can indicate ovulation, although not all women experience them:

  • Changes in body temperature:
    Your basal body temperature (BBT) may rise slightly during ovulation. Charting your BBT with a special thermometer can help you track your most fertile days.

  • Changes in cervical mucus:
    The consistency of cervical mucus becomes clearer, thinner, and more slippery, resembling egg whites.

  • Ovulation pain:
    Some women may feel a slight twinge or mild cramps in the lower abdomen, known as mittelschmerz.

  • Increased libido:
    Your sex drive may boost a couple of days before ovulation due to increased testosterone production.

  • Vulva changes:
    The outer part of your genitalia, called the labia, may swell.

  • Breast tenderness:
    Hormonal fluctuations can cause breast tenderness and sore nipples.

  • Bloating and fluid retention:
    Hormonal surges during ovulation can lead to bloating, water retention, gas, and slower digestion.

  • Mood changes:
    Some women may feel happier and in a better mood around ovulation.

  • Appetite changes:
    Your appetite may decrease slightly right before ovulation and increase a bit afterward.

  • Heightened sense of smell:
    Some women may experience a sharper sense of smell during ovulation, similar to what happens during pregnancy.

Remember that these signs vary from woman to woman, and not everyone experiences all of them. Tracking these symptoms can help you identify your most fertile days and increase your chances of conceiving if you're trying to get pregnant.


When does ovulation occur? ▼

Ovulation usually occurs around the middle of your menstrual cycle, which is typically on day 14 of a 28-day cycle. However, this can vary from woman to woman. As a general rule, ovulation takes place approximately 13 to 14 days before your next expected period.

It's important to note that menstrual cycles can vary in length, ranging from 23 to 35 days, and the timing of ovulation may vary slightly from month to month. If you have regular and predictable periods, estimating the time of ovulation is usually straightforward. However, if your periods are irregular, unpredictable, or occur less frequently than every 40 days, tracking ovulation can be more challenging.

To help determine your fertile window and ovulation date, you can use tools such as the Ovulation Calculator on this web application Mom & Me. These resources can provide you with an estimate based on the information you provide about your menstrual cycle.


How long does ovulation last? ▼

Ovulation lasts for a relatively short time. After the release of an egg from the ovary, it can be fertilized for about 12 to 24 hours. This happens approximately 12 to 36 hours after a surge in the hormone LH.

If you're trying to get pregnant, it's best to have intercourse every other day starting four to five days before the predicted ovulation day until the day of ovulation. Having sex within this timeframe gives you the highest chance of conception. However, it is still possible to get pregnant if you have sex within the 24 hours after predicted ovulation.

On average, healthy couples who are not using birth control have a 25 to 30 percent chance of getting pregnant during each monthly cycle. The likelihood of conception decreases as women reach their mid-30s and varies based on individual circumstances. Having intercourse on the day of ovulation improves the odds of getting pregnant.

It's important to remember that it only takes one sperm to fertilize an egg and achieve pregnancy. This means that you can still get pregnant if you have sex up to five days before ovulation or one day after ovulation.


What is hormone LH and what does it do? ▼

LH stands for luteinizing hormone. It is a hormone produced by the pituitary gland in the brain. LH plays a crucial role in the menstrual cycle and ovulation.

During the menstrual cycle, LH levels rise in a process called the LH surge. This surge triggers the release of a mature egg from the ovary, known as ovulation. Ovulation usually occurs around 12 to 36 hours after the LH surge.

By tracking LH levels, either through ovulation predictor kits or with the help of medical professionals, women can determine when they are approaching ovulation. This knowledge can be helpful for couples who are trying to conceive as it indicates the most fertile period of the menstrual cycle.


How do I track my ovulation? ▼

Here are some ways to track ovulation and determine when you are most likely to ovulate:

  • Chart your menstrual cycle:
    Keep track of your menstrual cycles for a few months to understand your pattern. You can use tools like a menstrual cycle calendar or ovulation calculators to help identify your most fertile days.

  • Listen to your body:
    Pay attention to signs of ovulation such as light cramps, changes in cervical mucus (discharge), breast tenderness, increased libido, mood changes, and other physical sensations.

  • Track basal body temperature (BBT):
    Measure your body temperature using a special thermometer each morning before getting out of bed. Your BBT may slightly rise after ovulation, indicating that you have already ovulated. This method helps you identify ovulation after it happens, but it may not predict it in advance.

  • Pay attention to cervical mucus:
    Notice changes in your cervical mucus throughout your cycle. Before ovulation, the mucus becomes thinner, clearer, and more slippery, similar to egg whites. This fertile cervical mucus helps sperm reach the egg.

  • Use ovulation predictor kits (OPKs):
    These kits detect the surge in luteinizing hormone (LH) levels, which occurs a day or two before ovulation. OPKs can provide more precise timing of ovulation. You can pee on a stick and wait for the results to indicate if you are about to ovulate.

  • Consider other methods:
    Some less common methods include saliva tests and chloride ion surge tests, which detect changes in saliva or sweat to predict ovulation. These methods are not as widely used and may be less reliable.

Remember, everyone's body is different, and these methods may vary in effectiveness for different individuals. It's important to find the method that works best for you and consult with a healthcare professional if you have any concerns or questions about tracking ovulation.


What does ovulation feel like? ▼

Moms who are trying to conceive often discuss the signs they notice when they're ovulating. Here's what some of them have shared:

  • Some moms feel pain during ovulation, usually on one side of their lower abdomen. It can be a sharp or cramp-like pain near the ovary area and uterus.

  • Many women notice changes in their cervical mucus during ovulation. It becomes thick, slippery, and similar to egg whites.

  • Some women don't experience any noticeable symptoms during ovulation, and that doesn't mean they can't get pregnant.

  • Remember, getting pregnant isn't guaranteed even if you're ovulating. It's important to be patient and persistent while trying to conceive.

If you're trying to get pregnant, paying attention to these signs can help you plan romantic activities during your most fertile days. Good luck and enjoy the process!


What is Anovulation? ▼

Having trouble getting pregnant? One possible reason could be anovulation, which means your ovaries are not releasing an egg during your menstrual cycle. If you don't ovulate, you can't conceive during that cycle. The good news is that there are treatments available to help trigger ovulation and improve your chances of getting pregnant.

Anovulation can be caused by different factors such as polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS), hormonal imbalances, ovarian insufficiency, high prolactin levels, or thyroid dysfunction. Symptoms may include irregular periods, light or heavy bleeding, and other related signs like acne, weight gain, or abnormal hair growth.

If you're not ovulating, it can make it difficult to time intercourse for conception. However, various treatments can help regulate hormone levels and stimulate ovulation, increasing your chances of getting pregnant. Medications like clomiphene citrate or letrozole can be used for PCOS and other conditions. In some cases, assisted reproductive techniques like in vitro fertilization (IVF) or using donor eggs may be recommended.

If you suspect anovulation or have irregular cycles, it's important to consult with your doctor. They can diagnose the condition, identify the underlying cause, and recommend appropriate treatments. Don't hesitate to seek medical advice, as many cases of anovulation can be successfully treated to improve your chances of conceiving.






Fertility and Infertility

Some important facts you should know.


What is fertilization and where does it occur? ▼

When a man's sperm meets a woman's egg, it happens in a special place called the fallopian tubes. The sperm has to swim from the vagina, through the cervix, and into the uterus to reach the fallopian tubes. If one sperm successfully joins with the egg, it's called fertilization. Then, the fertilized egg travels from the fallopian tubes to the uterus, where it attaches to the uterine wall and starts growing into a baby.

Sometimes, the fertilized egg may implant and grow in the wrong place, like the fallopian tube or other areas outside the uterus. This is called an ectopic pregnancy, which is not safe and needs medical attention.

So, fertilization happens in the fallopian tubes, and the fertilized egg then moves to the uterus to develop into a baby, but it's important for it to be in the right place for a healthy pregnancy.


When does fertilization occur? ▼

Fertilization occurs during a specific timeframe, usually within a few days around the time of ovulation. Ovulation is when an egg is released from the ovary.

For fertilization to happen, a sperm needs to be present in the fallopian tube at the same time as the egg. However, even if a sperm reaches the fallopian tube, it doesn't guarantee fertilization. The timing must be just right, and the sperm needs to meet the egg during this small window of opportunity. Sometimes, factors like fertility issues or timing can affect the chances of fertilization.

It's important to remember that fertilization usually happens in only one of the fallopian tubes, where the egg is present during a given month.


When am I most fertile? ▼

The time when you are most likely to get pregnant is during the 5 days before ovulation, including the day you ovulate. This is because sperm can survive inside your body for up to 5 days, so if you have sex during this time, the sperm can fertilize an egg if it is released.

After ovulation, which is when an egg is released from your ovary, the egg can only survive for about 12 to 24 hours. Once this time has passed, you cannot get pregnant until your next menstrual cycle begins.

Your highest chances of getting pregnant occur in the 3 days leading up to and including the day of ovulation.


How long does it take for a sperm to fertilize an egg? ▼

Fertilization is not an instant process and takes some time. After sex, it takes about an hour for sperm to reach the egg. However, the entire fertilization process can take several hours.

Initially, after sex, the semen coagulates in the vagina, forming a barrier that keeps the sperm from moving too far in the wrong direction. Within 30 minutes, the semen liquefies, and any sperm that haven't reached the cervix are eliminated by the vagina's acidity.

The sperm that make it to the cervical canal undergo changes and gain the speed needed to swim through the uterus and fallopian tubes. The cervical mucus becomes more favorable for sperm movement during fertile times.

During ovulation, the cervical mucus becomes stretchy, clear, and thin, helping sperm travel efficiently. The mucus forms tiny tracks that sperm can ride on to reach the egg.

When sperm reach the egg, they face tough competition from other sperm. Hundreds of sperm surround the egg, attempting to penetrate its outer layer. Only one sperm can successfully enter the egg, triggering a chemical reaction that prevents other sperm from entering. The genetic material from the sperm and egg then combines, resulting in fertilization.

Overall, the journey and competition between sperm to fertilize an egg can take several hours.


What is Infertility? ▼

Infertility is defined as the inability to conceive a pregnancy or carry a pregnancy to full term after having regular, unprotected sexual intercourse for at least one year (or six months for women over 35) without the use of contraception. It is a medical condition that affects both men and women and can be caused by various factors.

In women, infertility may be caused by issues such as hormonal imbalances, problems with ovulation, blocked fallopian tubes, or structural abnormalities in the reproductive organs. Age, certain medical conditions (such as polycystic ovary syndrome or endometriosis), and lifestyle factors (such as smoking, excessive alcohol consumption, or being significantly underweight or overweight) can also contribute to female infertility.

In men, infertility can result from issues with sperm production, sperm quality or movement, or blockages that prevent the release of sperm. Factors such as hormonal imbalances, genetic conditions, certain medications, and lifestyle choices (like smoking or excessive alcohol use) can impact male fertility.

Infertility can be a challenging and emotionally distressing experience for individuals and couples who desire to have a child. However, with advancements in medical technology and fertility treatments, many couples are able to overcome infertility and achieve a successful pregnancy. If you suspect you or your partner may be experiencing fertility issues, it is recommended to consult with a healthcare professional who specializes in reproductive medicine.


what are the causes of infertility? ▼

Infertility can have various causes, and it can affect both men and women. Here are some common causes of infertility:

Causes of Female Infertility:

  • Ovulation problems:
    Irregular or absent ovulation can make it difficult to conceive.

  • Hormonal imbalances:
    Imbalances in hormones like estrogen and progesterone can affect fertility.

  • Structural issues:
    Abnormalities or blockages in the fallopian tubes or uterus can prevent the egg from being fertilized or implanting.

  • Endometriosis:
    A condition where the tissue that normally lines the uterus grows outside of it, leading to fertility problems.

  • Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS):
    A hormonal disorder that affects ovulation and can cause infertility.

  • Age:
    As women get older, their fertility declines, especially after the age of 35.

Causes of Male Infertility:
  • Low sperm count:
    A low number of sperm can reduce the chances of fertilizing an egg.

  • Poor sperm motility:
    When sperm cannot swim well, they have difficulty reaching and fertilizing the egg.

  • Structural issues:
    Blockages or abnormalities in the male reproductive system can prevent the release of sperm.

  • Hormonal imbalances:
    Imbalances in hormones like testosterone can affect sperm production.

  • Varicocele:
    Enlarged veins in the scrotum can impact sperm production.

  • Lifestyle factors:
    Factors like smoking, excessive alcohol consumption, drug use, and certain medications can affect sperm health.

Other Factors:
  • Unexplained infertility:
    In some cases, the cause of infertility cannot be identified.

  • Genetic factors:
    Certain genetic conditions can impact fertility in both men and women.

  • Environmental factors:
    Exposure to toxins, radiation, or certain chemicals can affect fertility.

It's important to remember that infertility can be a complex issue with multiple factors involved. If you're experiencing difficulty getting pregnant, it's best to consult with a healthcare professional who specializes in fertility to determine the underlying cause and explore possible treatments.


What is endometriosis? ▼

Endometriosis is a condition where tissue similar to the lining of the uterus grows in other places in the body. It can cause pain and make it harder for a woman to get pregnant.

Endometriosis can affect any girl or woman who menstruates, but it's more common in women in their 30s and 40s. The exact cause is unknown, but it may be related to hormonal and immune system issues.

Some common symptoms of endometriosis include severe period cramps, lower back or pelvic pain, pain during or after sex, and digestive problems. It can also cause fertility problems and make it harder to conceive.

While it can be more challenging to get pregnant with endometriosis, it's still possible. About 40% of women with infertility issues have endometriosis. When a woman with endometriosis does get pregnant, she may have a higher chance of needing a C-section.

There's no cure for endometriosis, but treatments are available to manage symptoms and improve fertility. Medications and surgery can be used to remove growths and improve the chances of getting pregnant.

Having endometriosis may increase the risk of certain pregnancy complications, such as placenta previa, miscarriage, preterm birth, and preeclampsia. However, with regular prenatal care, many women with endometriosis go on to have healthy pregnancies and babies.

If you have concerns about endometriosis and pregnancy, it's important to talk to your doctor. They can provide guidance and recommend appropriate treatments to support your reproductive health.


What is Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)? ▼

Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) is a hormonal condition that affects ovulation. It can make it more difficult for women to get pregnant and increases the risk of certain pregnancy complications.

PCOS is caused by factors like genetics, higher levels of male hormones, excess insulin in the body, and being overweight or obese.

Common symptoms of PCOS include irregular menstrual cycles, acne, excessive hair growth, thinning hair, weight gain, and insulin resistance.

While there is no cure for PCOS, a healthy lifestyle can help manage the symptoms. Eating a balanced diet, losing weight if necessary, and exercising can improve ovulation and regulate periods.

Women with PCOS can still get pregnant with or without the help of medications that promote ovulation or assisted reproductive techniques like in vitro fertilization (IVF).

PCOS increases the risk of complications such as miscarriage and gestational diabetes during pregnancy. Women with PCOS are also more likely to develop preeclampsia and may have a slightly higher chance of needing a cesarean section (C-section).

To increase the chances of getting pregnant with PCOS, medications like metformin, clomiphene citrate (Clomid), or letrozole (Femara) can be used to stimulate ovulation. In more severe cases, IVF may be recommended.

With proper care and treatment, women with PCOS can still conceive and have a healthy pregnancy. It's important to work closely with healthcare professionals to manage the condition and address any concerns.


What are Fibroid Tumours? ▼

Uterine fibroids are common growths in the uterus that are usually harmless. They affect up to 20% of women of childbearing age and are made of muscle and fibrous tissue.

Fibroids may cause symptoms like heavy or painful periods, bloating, frequent urination, and pain during sex. During pregnancy, they can lead to pain, vaginal bleeding, and pelvic pressure.

The exact cause of fibroids is unknown, but hormones and genetics may play a role. They are more common in Black women and can be influenced by factors like obesity and diet.

Fibroids rarely cause fertility issues, but in some cases, they can make it harder to get pregnant. They can affect the fallopian tubes, cervix, or uterus, making it difficult for sperm to reach and fertilize an egg. Treatment can improve the chances of conception.

During pregnancy, fibroids are usually not a problem. They may grow due to increased hormone levels but often shrink after giving birth. Fibroids slightly increase the risk of preterm delivery and may require a C-section.

Most fibroids don't require treatment during pregnancy and can be managed with rest and pain medications. Surgery is only performed in severe cases.

It's important to keep your doctor informed about fibroids, especially if you experience symptoms or have fertility concerns. They can monitor the situation and provide appropriate care if needed.


Irregular periods and pregnancy ▼

Irregular periods can be a bit unpredictable, with your menstrual cycles happening at unusual intervals or unpredictable times. They can be caused by temporary factors like stress, travel, or changes in your routine. Chronic irregular periods can be due to factors like weight changes, hormonal imbalances, or conditions like polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS).

Having irregular periods doesn't always mean you can't get pregnant, but it can make it harder. When your periods are irregular, it's difficult to track ovulation and know the best time to try to conceive. Irregular periods can sometimes indicate a problem with ovulation, which can affect fertility.

If you're trying to conceive or if irregular periods bother you, it's a good idea to see a doctor. They can evaluate your hormone levels and suggest treatments to regulate your menstrual cycle or address any underlying issues. Fertility drugs may be recommended if you're trying to get pregnant.

Having irregular periods doesn't mean you can't have a healthy pregnancy and baby. Whether you're trying to conceive or not, it's important to consult with your doctor to ensure nothing is being missed and to discuss the best options for you.


Understanding Male Infertility ▼

Infertility can be a challenge for couples trying to have a baby, and it's not just a problem for women. Men can also experience fertility issues. In fact, about one-third of infertility cases are due to male factors.

Male infertility can be caused by various factors. It could be because the man's body doesn't produce enough sperm, the sperm doesn't function properly, or there is a blockage preventing the sperm from reaching the egg. These issues can be related to health conditions, hormonal imbalances, certain medications, lifestyle factors like smoking or obesity, and exposure to toxins.

Getting a diagnosis for male infertility usually involves a semen analysis, where the sperm is examined for count, shape, and movement. Further tests may be recommended to determine the underlying cause.

Male infertility can affect a couple's ability to conceive if the sperm is not able to fertilize the egg. However, with some treatment options, such as surgery or medication, fertility can be improved. Assisted reproductive therapies like intrauterine insemination or in vitro fertilization may also be used. Lifestyle changes, such as quitting smoking or losing weight, can also make a difference.

It's important to remember that many couples dealing with male infertility are still able to conceive, either naturally or with the help of treatments. Seeking medical advice and discussing the available options with a healthcare professional is recommended for couples facing male infertility.


Available treatments for fertility issues ▼

In Sri Lanka, there are various treatments available for fertility issues. Here are some common treatments that may be offered:

  • Medications:
    Doctors can give medicines to help balance hormones and stimulate ovulation in women who are having trouble getting pregnant.

  • Intrauterine Insemination (IUI):
    During this procedure, the doctor puts specially prepared sperm directly into the woman's uterus to increase the chances of getting pregnant.

  • In Vitro Fertilization (IVF):
    IVF is a more advanced treatment where eggs are taken from the woman's ovaries and combined with sperm in a lab. The fertilized eggs are then placed back into the woman's uterus.

  • Intracytoplasmic Sperm Injection (ICSI):
    This is a special type of IVF where a single sperm is injected directly into an egg to help with fertilization. It is used when there are issues with the man's sperm.

  • Assisted Hatching:
    During IVF, a small hole can be made in the outer shell of the embryo to help it attach to the uterus.

  • Gamete Intrafallopian Transfer (GIFT) and Zygote Intrafallopian Transfer (ZIFT):
    These procedures involve transferring eggs and sperm or fertilized embryos into the fallopian tubes instead of the uterus. They are not as common as IVF.

  • Donor Egg or Sperm:
    In some cases, when there are major fertility issues or genetic concerns, eggs or sperm from someone else can be used for fertilization.

It's important to talk to a fertility specialist who can explain these treatments in more detail and help determine the best option for each couple. They will provide guidance, answer questions, and support throughout the process.






pre-pregnancy Health

Some important facts you should know.


Preconception Counseling ▼

When you're ready to have a baby, it's a good idea to schedule a preconception counseling appointment with your healthcare provider or midwife. This appointment usually takes place about three months before you start trying to conceive.

During preconception counseling, your provider will review various aspects of your health and your partner's health. They will ask about your medical history, including any chronic health conditions you have and medications you're currently taking. It's important to get any medical conditions under control before getting pregnant. You should also inform your provider about any previous surgeries or complications in past pregnancies.

Your family medical history will also be assessed to see if there are any genetic conditions that could be passed on to your child. Some genetic disorders can be identified through blood tests before pregnancy, so it's important to talk to your doctor about genetic screening.

Your vaccine status will be checked to ensure you're up to date on important vaccines like rubella and varicella. Getting these vaccines during pregnancy can be risky, so it's best to have them at least a month before trying to conceive. It's also recommended to stay up to date on your annual flu vaccine, which is safe during pregnancy.

If you've been exposed to viruses like Zika, Dengue or COVID-19, it's important to inform your provider as these infections can have serious health risks for both you and your baby.

Lastly, your provider may screen for intimate partner violence (IPV). This involves asking about any physical, sexual, or emotional abuse caused by a current or former partner. Identifying IPV is important for your well-being and safety.

By discussing all these aspects, preconception counseling helps ensure that you and your partner are in good health and prepared for a healthy pregnancy and baby.


Pre-pregnancy Checkup ▼

Before you plan to get pregnant, it's a good idea to have a checkup with your healthcare provider. During this pre-pregnancy checkup, they will perform various tests and exams to ensure your health and address any potential concerns. Here are some important details to consider, especially for individuals in Sri Lanka:

  • Blood tests:
    Your blood will be tested to determine your blood type and check for any sexually transmitted infections (STIs). It's crucial to identify and treat any infections before getting pregnant to protect both you and your baby.

  • Pelvic exam:
    Your healthcare provider will examine your pelvic organs, including the vagina, cervix, uterus, and ovaries, to ensure they are healthy and functioning properly. This exam helps detect any abnormalities or conditions that may affect your ability to conceive or carry a pregnancy.

  • Pap smear:
    A Pap smear is a test to screen for cervical cancer. It involves collecting a sample of cells from your cervix for analysis. Regular Pap smears are essential for early detection and treatment of cervical cancer, if present.

  • Physical exam:
    During the physical exam, your healthcare provider will assess your overall health. They will measure your weight, check your blood pressure, pulse rate, body temperature, and breathing rate. These measurements help determine if you are in good health to support a pregnancy.

  • Reviewing vaccination status:

  • Discussing mosquito-borne diseases:

How to prepare your body for pregnancy? ▼

Here are some simple steps you can take to prepare your body for pregnancy and ensure you're in good health before trying to conceive:

  • Avoid alcohol:
    Drinking alcohol can affect your fertility and harm the baby if you become pregnant. It's best to avoid alcohol altogether.

  • Eat a healthy diet:
    Focus on eating a balanced diet that includes plenty of fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean proteins, and low-fat dairy. Avoid processed and fatty foods. Limit your caffeine intake to one to two cups of coffee per day.

  • Stay away from harmful substances:
    Try to avoid exposure to toxic chemicals, such as lead and pesticides, as well as radiation, like X-rays. These substances can be harmful to the developing baby.

  • Exercise regularly and maintain a healthy weight:
    Engage in regular exercise and aim to maintain a healthy weight. Being overweight or underweight can increase the risk of complications during pregnancy.

  • Address domestic violence:
    If you're experiencing domestic violence or abuse, seek help from healthcare providers or support services to ensure your safety and well-being.

  • Quit smoking:
    If you smoke, it's important to quit before getting pregnant. Smoking can harm both you and your baby, leading to various complications and health issues.

  • Reduce the risk of infections:
    Take precautions to avoid foodborne illnesses by avoiding undercooked meat and raw eggs. Also, minimize contact with animal feces and cat litter, as they can contain harmful parasites or viruses. Wash your hands frequently and stay away from sick individuals.

  • Take prenatal vitamins:
    Start taking a prenatal vitamin or a daily vitamin that contains at least 400 micrograms of folic acid. Folic acid helps reduce the risk of certain birth defects in the baby's brain and spinal cord.

By following these steps, you can optimize your health and increase the chances of a healthy pregnancy when you're ready to conceive. Remember to consult with your healthcare provider for personalized advice and guidance.


Managing Stress and Anxiety while trying to conceive ▼

When a couple is trying to conceive and it takes longer than expected, it can be stressful and cause anxiety. The process of trying to conceive can become overwhelming, leading to obsessive behaviors and constant testing. The anticipation during ovulation and the two-week wait until pregnancy testing can cause a rollercoaster of emotions.

Dealing with fertility challenges can be emotionally draining and may lead to feelings of frustration, sadness, and decreased self-esteem. The pressure from others and well-meaning comments can make the situation more difficult. It's important to recognize and address these emotions and seek support.

To manage stress and anxiety, it's helpful to establish healthier strategies. Setting limits on ovulation and pregnancy testing can reduce anxiety. Engaging in activities unrelated to pregnancy, such as pursuing hobbies or spending time with loved ones, can provide a welcome distraction. It's also essential to focus on gratitude and appreciate the positive aspects of life.

Accepting that some things are beyond control and trusting that everything will work out can bring peace of mind. Remember that seeking professional help and consulting a fertility specialist is an option if needed.


Pregnancy Symptoms ▼

When trying to conceive, you may experience symptoms that make you wonder if you're pregnant. However, it can be challenging to distinguish between early pregnancy symptoms and premenstrual syndrome (PMS) because they can be similar.

Pregnancy symptoms can vary from person to person and may start at different times. On average, here's a timeline of common pregnancy symptoms based on a 28-day menstrual cycle:

  • About 14 days after your last period:
    Conception occurs.

  • 17 or more days after your last period:
    Early symptoms like sensitivity to smell, tender breasts, fatigue, and raised basal body temperature may begin.

  • 20 to 26 days after your last period:
    Some women may experience implantation bleeding (light spotting) and notice changes in cervical mucus.

  • 28 to 35 days after your last period:
    Frequent urination and mood swings may begin, and you may miss your period.

  • 35 or more days after your last period:
    Other pregnancy symptoms may start during the first trimester.

Early pregnancy symptoms can include a missed period, fatigue, raised basal body temperature, heightened sense of smell, tender and swollen breasts, spotting (implantation bleeding), changes in cervical mucus, frequent urination, mood swings, bloating, heartburn, nausea (morning sickness), food aversions, and excess saliva.

These symptoms can occur before a missed period, but not everyone experiences all of them, and some may not have any symptoms until later in pregnancy.

Some early signs of pregnancy include a rise in basal body temperature, increased sensitivity to smell, breast changes, fatigue, implantation bleeding, changes in cervical mucus, frequent urination, mood swings, bloating, heartburn, and morning sickness.

It's important to note that these symptoms can also be caused by other factors, so the only way to confirm pregnancy is through a home pregnancy test or a blood test conducted by a doctor.

Home pregnancy tests typically detect the hormone hCG in your urine, which is produced by the placenta after implantation. For accurate results, it's best to wait until your period is expected or slightly later to take the test.

If you suspect you might be pregnant, it's a good idea to schedule an appointment with your OB/GYN for confirmation and to start prenatal care.
