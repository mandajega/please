Prenatal Vitamins

Why do they matter & how do we choose?

Eating a healthy diet is important to get the vitamins and minerals your body needs. However, during pregnancy, you may not get enough of certain nutrients. If you are pregnant or planning to have a baby, taking prenatal vitamins can help make sure you get all the necessary nutrients. They fill any gaps in your diet and support a healthy pregnancy.


What are prenatal vitamins? ▼

Prenatal vitamins are special supplements made for pregnant women to provide them with the vitamins and minerals their bodies need for a healthy pregnancy. Your doctor may recommend taking them when you start planning for a baby and throughout your pregnancy.

While it's important to eat a healthy diet, taking a prenatal vitamin can help make sure you get all the nutrients you and your baby need, especially during pregnancy. It helps fill any nutritional gaps in your diet and supports a healthy pregnancy.


What to look for in prenatal vitamins? ▼

Prenatal vitamins are special supplements for pregnant women. They provide vitamins and minerals that are important for a healthy pregnancy. Your doctor may recommend taking them when you plan to have a baby and throughout your pregnancy.

When choosing prenatal vitamins, look for ones that contain specific nutrients:

  • Folic acid:
    This helps prevent birth defects in the baby's brain and spinal cord. It's recommended to take 400 micrograms daily, starting before pregnancy and during the first 12 weeks.

  • Calcium:
    Important for the mother's bone health and the baby's bone growth.

  • Iodine:
    Essential for a healthy thyroid function during pregnancy.

  • Iron:
    Helps make more red blood cells, which carry oxygen to the baby.

It's important to take prenatal vitamins even if you eat a healthy diet. These vitamins help ensure that you and your baby get all the necessary nutrients for a healthy pregnancy.


Why are prenatal vitamins important? ▼

Prenatal vitamins are important during pregnancy because they provide extra folic acid and iron that your body needs. Here's why they are important:

Folic acid helps prevent serious problems with the baby's brain and spinal cord. Taking folic acid before pregnancy and during early pregnancy is crucial for the baby's development.

Iron supports the growth of the placenta and the baby. It helps your body make more blood to supply oxygen to the baby. Iron also helps prevent anemia, which is when your blood doesn't have enough healthy red blood cells.

By taking prenatal vitamins, you can ensure that you and your baby get enough of these important nutrients for a healthy pregnancy.


Which prenatal vitamin is the best? ▼

Prenatal vitamins can be purchased without a prescription at most pharmacies. Your healthcare provider may recommend a specific brand or allow you to choose one yourself.

When looking for a prenatal vitamin, it's important to check for folic acid and iron. These are crucial for your baby's development. It's also beneficial to find a prenatal vitamin that contains calcium and vitamin D, as they help with the growth of your baby's teeth and bones. Other nutrients to consider include vitamin C, vitamin A, vitamin E, B vitamins, zinc, and iodine.

Depending on your situation, your healthcare provider might suggest higher doses of certain nutrients. For instance, if you have previously had a baby with a neural tube defect, they might recommend a separate supplement with a higher dose of folic acid before and during future pregnancies.

It's important to avoid taking additional prenatal vitamins or multivitamins with excessive dosages. Too much of certain vitamins can be harmful to your baby. For example, taking too much vitamin A during pregnancy can potentially cause harm.

Remember to consult with your healthcare provider to determine the best prenatal vitamin for you and to ensure you are taking the appropriate dosage.


Do I need to be concerned about other nutrients? ▼

There are other nutrients that can be beneficial for a healthy pregnancy. Your doctor can help you decide if you need additional supplements that include:

  • Omega-3 fatty acids:
    These fats are found in certain foods like fatty fish and nuts. They can lower the risk of preterm birth and having a baby with low birth weight. This also helps to promote a baby's brain development. If you don't consume enough omega-3s from your diet, your doctor may recommend a supplement.

  • Choline:
    While your body can produce some choline on its own, you mostly get it from food sources like beef, pork, chicken, fish, and eggs. Many pregnant women don't get enough choline, which is important for the baby's brain development.


When should I start taking prenatal vitamins? ▼

The best time to start taking prenatal vitamins is before getting pregnant. It's especially important to take folic acid at least one month before trying to conceive to prevent birth defects.

Some doctors recommend that all women who could become pregnant take prenatal vitamins, even if they are not planning a pregnancy.

Another reason as to why starting prenatal vitamins before getting pregnant is ideal is due to the fact that the The baby's neural tube, which develops into the brain and spinal cord, forms in the first month of pregnancy, sometimes before you even know you're pregnant. So taking prenatal vitamins in advance is a good practice for women of reproductive age.

Another important fact is that your growing baby relies on your body for nutrients, which can lead to deficiencies in vitamins and minerals during pregnancy. By starting prenatal vitamins before pregnancy, you can build up a reserve of these essential nutrients in your body. This way, you can ensure that both you and your baby have enough of these vitamins and minerals to support a healthy pregnancy. It's like preparing in advance and having extra resources to share with your baby as they grow inside you.


Do prenatal vitamins have any side effects? ▼

Some prenatal vitamins can cause nausea in pregnant women who are already experiencing morning sickness. If you experience this, talk to your healthcare provider. They may prescribe a different type of prenatal vitamin that is easier to take, such as chewables or liquids.

The iron in prenatal vitamins can also lead to constipation. To alleviate constipation, you can:

  • Eat foods high in fiber.

  • Drink plenty of water.

  • Engage in exercise if approved by your doctor.

  • Take a stool softener with your doctor's approval.

If these suggestions don't work, consult your healthcare provider for further options and advice.


Are there any side effects of taking prenatals while not pregnant? ▼

Taking prenatal vitamins when you're not pregnant generally does not cause harmful side effects. In fact, if there were negative effects, pregnant women wouldn't be advised to take them.

However, prenatal vitamins are specifically formulated with higher levels of certain nutrients needed during pregnancy. This means they may not be the best choice for non-pregnant individuals to take long-term.

For example, prenatal vitamins contain more iron than regular multivitamins. While taking excess iron may cause temporary side effects like constipation and nausea, it could become problematic over time.

In summary, if you're not pregnant or planning a pregnancy, you can wait until you actually need prenatal vitamins (a few months before pregnancy, during pregnancy, and often during breastfeeding) before starting to take them.


Are there any extra benefits? ▼

Some people believe that prenatal vitamins can provide extra benefits beyond supporting a healthy pregnancy. For example, they may contain biotin, a B vitamin that some claim promotes hair, nail, and skin growth. However, scientific studies have not found solid evidence to support these claims.

Prenatal vitamins with DHA, which is an omega-3 fatty acid, may contribute to the development of the baby's brain and eyes. They may also contain iodine, which helps with the baby's nervous system development.

Additionally, there is some research suggesting that taking prenatal vitamins may increase the chances of getting pregnant. However, it's important to note that prenatal vitamins are not a guaranteed solution for infertility issues, and getting pregnant involves various factors such as exercise, healthy diet, and avoiding substances like alcohol and drugs.

In summary, while prenatal vitamins have specific benefits for pregnancy, their potential extra benefits for hair, skin, fertility, etc., are not supported by strong scientific evidence.


What should you look for in a daily prenatal? ▼

Before purchasing a prenatal vitamin, consider the following factors:

  • Regulatory oversight:
    Look for prenatal vitamins that have been certified or approved by relevant regulatory bodies in Sri Lanka, such as the National Medicines Regulatory Authority (NMRA). This ensures that the health and ingredient claims have been verified.

  • Doses:
    Check that the prenatal vitamin contains appropriate amounts of key nutrients recommended for pregnant women in Sri Lanka. This may include folic acid, iron, calcium, vitamin D, vitamin C, and iodine. Consult with your healthcare provider or refer to local guidelines for specific recommendations.

  • Availability:
    Consider the accessibility and availability of the prenatal vitamin in Sri Lanka. Check if it can be easily obtained from local pharmacies, supermarkets, or healthcare facilities. It's important to choose a vitamin that you can consistently obtain throughout your pregnancy.

  • Cultural considerations:
    Take into account any cultural preferences or dietary restrictions that may affect your choice of prenatal vitamin. For example, if you follow a vegetarian or vegan diet, ensure that the vitamin does not contain animal-derived ingredients.

  • Healthcare provider's guidance:
    Consult your healthcare provider, such as an obstetrician or midwife, for personalized advice on choosing a prenatal vitamin that suits your specific needs and circumstances. They can consider your individual health status and provide recommendations based on local practices and guidelines.

Remember, prenatal vitamins are an important supplement during pregnancy, but it's always best to consult with a healthcare professional to ensure you choose the most suitable option for you.


Tips for taking prenatal vitamins ▼

  • If your prenatal vitamins are making you feel sick, talk to your doctor about trying a different brand that may be easier on your stomach.

  • Consider different forms of prenatal vitamins, such as gummies or beverages, as they may be easier to digest. You can also split your doses throughout the day to reduce any discomfort.

  • Stay hydrated by drinking plenty of water before and after taking your prenatal vitamins. This can help with constipation. If needed, ask your doctor about adding a fiber supplement to your diet.

  • Experiment with taking your prenatal vitamins with or without food to see which works best for you. Some people find it helpful to take them with a meal, while others prefer taking them on an empty stomach.

Remember, if you have any concerns or questions about taking prenatal vitamins, consult with your healthcare provider for personalized advice.
